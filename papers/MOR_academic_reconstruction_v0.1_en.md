# Microscopic Observational Relativity

## An Ontological Reconstruction of Relative Facts, Unmanifested Domains, and Slice Invariants in Relational Quantum Mechanics

Academic reconstruction draft academic-v0.1 | English edition | GitHub first public version | 2026-06-30

## Abstract

This paper repositions Microscopic Observational Relativity (MOR) as an ontological interpretive framework for quantum mechanics, rather than as an alternative dynamical theory intended to replace standard quantum mechanics. It explicitly acknowledges substantial overlap with relational quantum mechanics (RQM), decoherence theory, quantum Darwinism, and quantum reference frame (QRF) research. Its aim is not to claim a wholly new physical theory, but to formulate three possible contributions within that existing landscape: the positive ontological status of unmanifested domains, a systematic distinction between observational slices and slice invariants, and the commitment that the microscopic relational domain may be prior to macroscopic space.

The central claim of this paper is that quantum measurement should not be understood as a physical rupture of complete reality, but as the factualization of a manifest slice within a given observational reference frame. Relative facts are not subjective states of knowledge; public facts are not reference-frame-free presentations of full-dimensional reality. Rather, they are layers of fact stabilized through decoherence, redundant environmental records, and a shared public projection plane. This paper also corrects several symbolic expressions used in earlier MOR drafts that were only heuristic, and proposes a more conservative formal sketch based on density operators, restrictions to observable algebras, the Lueders rule, and information loss in the transition from full state structure to observational slices and unmanifested domains.

This remains a conceptual weak version of the theory. It does not derive the Born rule, does not propose new experimental deviations, and does not claim to solve the full measurement problem. Its contribution is to offer a way of combining relational realism with an ontology of manifestation, and to provide a citable draft for future engagement with RQM, Stable Facts/Relative Facts, Wigner's friend, QRF, and spacetime-emergence literature.

Keywords: relational quantum mechanics; relative facts; decoherence; quantum Darwinism; quantum reference frames; quantum entanglement; ontology; spacetime emergence

## 1. Problem and Positioning

The measurement problem in quantum mechanics has long troubled both physics and philosophy. If quantum states evolve linearly, why do measurements yield single definite outcomes? If wavefunction collapse is merely a computational rule, what ontological picture corresponds to it? If facts are relative to observational reference frames, how does a stable objective world arise?

Earlier formulations of MOR attempted to address these questions using concepts such as observational slices, unmanifested domains, microscopic relational domains, and public projection planes. On closer examination, however, many of these intuitions are strongly related to existing literature, especially Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics, Di Biagio and Rovelli's distinction between relative facts and stable facts, Zurek's decoherence and quantum Darwinism, and recent work on quantum reference frames.

This draft therefore no longer presents MOR as a wholly new theory. Instead, it reconstructs MOR as an ontological proposal near RQM and decoherence/quantum Darwinism, with three highlighted additions: unmanifested domains, slice invariants, and spatial emergence.

> The central question of this paper is not whether MOR is entirely new, but whether MOR offers a meaningful ontological contribution within the landscape of relational interpretations of quantum mechanics.

## 2. The Original Intuition

The original intuition behind MOR did not arise from the derivation of a specific paper, but from a simple and persistent question: have we projected too much of our macroscopic experience of objects, space, properties, and observation onto the microscopic world? In ordinary macroscopic experience, we always read an object from a certain angle, through a certain channel, and by means of a certain mode of interaction. What we obtain is a slice of the object, not its full-dimensional reality.

This intuition becomes sharper in the quantum domain. A microscopic system should not be imagined as a tiny ball carrying a complete set of classical properties. A more appropriate picture is that the system is described by state structure in Hilbert space, projection relations, and channels of interaction. Observation does not reveal the object itself in full; it obtains a recordable projected slice within a particular reference frame.

The central force of MOR is therefore this: there is no ontological collapse, only slice factualization; there is no complete objective reality presented without reference frame, only a stable shared public projection plane; space need not be the fundamental stage, but may be an emergent manifestation of microscopic relational domains at the macroscopic level.

> In the ontological picture proposed by MOR, the microscopic ground should not first be understood as a set of mutually isolated entities, but as relational structure. Measurement is not interpreted as the collapse or rupture of global reality, but as the factualization of a particular observational slice. The macroscopic objective world is not a reference-frame-free display of full-dimensional reality, but a public projection plane formed through stable records, decoherence, and shared interaction channels.

## 3. Relation to Existing Literature

### 3.1 Relational Quantum Mechanics: The Close Proximity of Relative Facts

The core idea of RQM is that quantum states and facts are always relative to physical systems, rather than to a reference-frame-free global observer. MOR's notion of an observational slice is closely aligned with this idea: facts are not subjective mental states, but facts manifested relative to a system or observational reference frame through physical interaction.

The difference between MOR and RQM should not be overstated. A more defensible formulation is that MOR accepts the direction of relative facts in RQM, and attempts to add a positive ontological account of unfactualized structure, namely the unmanifested domain. MOR asks: when a particular slice becomes factual, how should the structure that does not manifest within that reference frame be understood? It cannot be reduced to subjective ignorance, nor can it be described as a set of local hidden variables.

### 3.2 Stable Facts, Decoherence, and the Public Projection Plane

Di Biagio and Rovelli's distinction between stable facts and relative facts is highly relevant to MOR's distinction between observational slices and the public projection plane. MOR should acknowledge that the public projection plane is not created from nothing; it is closely related to the idea that relative facts may become stable facts through decoherence.

Zurek's work on decoherence, environment-induced superselection, and quantum Darwinism further explains how the environment can redundantly record stable information, allowing multiple observers to extract consistent facts from different fragments of the environment. MOR's position is that decoherence and quantum Darwinism explain the physical stabilization mechanism of the public projection plane. MOR adds that the public projection plane, however stable and shared, should not be mistaken for complete reference-frame-free reality.

### 3.3 Quantum Reference Frames and the Burden of the Word "Relativity"

In physics, the word "relativity" usually implies reference-frame transformations and invariant structures. In earlier MOR drafts, the term was used mainly as a philosophical analogy; no rigorous transformation group for observational reference frames was supplied. If MOR is to retain the name "relativity" in an academic context, the most realistic route is to connect it with the literature on quantum reference frames.

The work of Giacomini, Castro-Ruiz, and Brukner on quantum reference frames shows that superposition, entanglement, and the form of physical laws can be discussed rigorously across different quantum reference frames. For MOR, QRF research offers a natural entry point for mathematically formulating the relativity of observational slices.

## 4. Three Proposed Contributions of MOR

### 4.1 Unmanifested Domains: The Ontological Status of Unfactualized Structure

The most important candidate contribution of MOR is the unmanifested domain. In earlier drafts, this was called the "occluded domain." The term "unmanifested domain" is preferable because it avoids suggesting that a completed classical object is merely being hidden behind a screen. In this paper, an unmanifested domain is not a collection of definite classical properties unknown to the observer, and not a world destroyed by measurement. It is relational structure that has not acquired factual manifestation within the current observational reference frame.

The risk of this concept is significant. If the unmanifested domain is interpreted as meaning that all possible results already exist in a classical way and are merely unobserved, then it collapses into a local hidden-variable picture and runs into the Bell and Kochen-Specker constraints. MOR must therefore insist that the unmanifested domain is relational and non-classical unfactualized structure, not a database of pre-existing classical answers.

### 4.2 Slices and Invariants: A Systematic Distinction in the Spirit of Relativity

The second candidate contribution of MOR is to distinguish between slice-dependent quantities and cross-slice invariants. A spin value along a particular direction, a position record in a particular measurement, or a macroscopic trajectory generally belongs to an observational slice. Charge, rest mass, spin magnitude, conserved quantities, group-representation invariants, and statistical type are closer to structural invariants across slices.

> Slices vary; invariants do not. Many classical properties belong to slices. Intrinsic properties belong to cross-slice structure.

This is where MOR comes closest to the spirit of relativity. Just as special relativity distinguishes coordinate-dependent quantities from Lorentz invariants, MOR may attempt to distinguish facts dependent on observational reference frames from structural invariants preserved across those frames.

### 4.3 The Microscopic Relational Domain Prior to Space

The third candidate contribution of MOR is the commitment that the microscopic relational domain may be prior to macroscopic space. MOR does not understand microscopic objects as tiny balls moving in a pre-given space. Instead, it understands quantum systems as relational domains composed of correlations, interaction channels, manifestability, causal constraints, and coarse-graining rules.

```text
R_Q = (A_Q, I_Q, P_Q, C_Q, K_Q)
```

Here, A_Q denotes correlation structure; I_Q denotes interaction channels; P_Q denotes projectable or manifestable structure; C_Q denotes causal reachability and no-signalling constraints; and K_Q denotes coarse-graining rules by which microscopic relational domains manifest as macroscopic space. This expression is only a conceptual coordinate system, not a mature physical equation.

## 5. A Revised Formal Sketch

Earlier MOR drafts used symbols such as Omega_Q, Sigma_M, and Lambda_M, some of which were only heuristic. An academic version must avoid presenting undefined symbols as if they were completed mathematics. This paper therefore adopts a more conservative formal sketch.

### 5.1 State and Observational Reference Frame

Let the quantum system Q be represented by a density operator rho on a Hilbert space H. An observational reference frame M may be approximated as a set of jointly measurable observables, or as a commutative algebra A_M. In the idealized case of projective measurement, it may also be represented by a set of projectors {P_i^M}.

```text
rho in D(H),    M -> A_M or {P_i^M}
```

### 5.2 Observational Slice

In algebraic language, an observational slice can be understood as the restriction of the state rho to the observable algebra A_M. It is not the full state rho itself, but the factual structure that is accessible, recordable, and comparable within the current observational reference frame.

```text
Sigma_M(rho) := rho|_{A_M}
```

In ideal projective measurement, the probability of outcome i is still given by the Born rule:

```text
p_i = Tr(P_i^M rho)
```

If outcome i has been recorded, the conditional state should be written according to the Lueders rule:

```text
rho_i' = P_i^M rho P_i^M / Tr(P_i^M rho)
```

For a composite system AB, if a local measurement is performed on A, the expression should be:

```text
rho_a' = (P_a^A tensor I_B) rho_AB (P_a^A tensor I_B) / Tr[(P_a^A tensor I_B) rho_AB]
```

This corrects an error in earlier drafts where the projector was written only on the left. A local measurement is not a signal sent from A to B. It is the conditioning of a correlated slice within a nonseparable joint state.

### 5.3 Unmanifested Domain

The early expression Lambda_M(Q) = Omega_Q - Sigma_M(Q) can only be treated as a conceptual diagram, not as a mathematical formula. A more cautious formulation is that the unmanifested domain refers to the structure not retained when the full state rho is restricted, coarse-grained, or decohered into the reference-frame-accessible structure Sigma_M(rho).

One candidate formal route is to introduce a decoherence or conditional-expectation map D_M associated with M, and to use the informational difference between rho and D_M(rho) as a measure of the degree of unmanifestation. Possible tools include relative entropy, measures of coherence, or information loss under algebraic restriction.

```text
Lambda_M: the information-loss structure generated by rho -> Sigma_M(rho) or rho -> D_M(rho)
```

This paper does not treat this as a finished mathematical definition. It is only a direction for future formalization.

## 6. A MOR Reconstruction of Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement is a central test case for MOR. MOR does not understand entanglement as superluminal communication between two spatially separated particles. It understands entanglement as the correlated manifestation of a joint microscopic relational domain within local observational slices.

```text
|Psi_AB> != |psi_A> tensor |psi_B>
```

This means that the complete information of an entangled system cannot be decomposed into the complete information of A plus the complete information of B. Macroscopic space presents A and B as spatially separated objects, but that spatial separation does not exhaust their nonseparable structure in the joint Hilbert space.

> Spatial separation is not relational separation. Positional independence is not ontological independence. A local record is not complete reality.

After A is measured, knowing A's result allows one to infer the corresponding result for B, but this is not controllable communication. B's local statistics remain indistinguishable without access to A's measurement setting and outcome. The joint correlation becomes an experimental fact on the public projection plane only after records are compared through classical communication.

In MOR, the violation of Bell inequalities is not interpreted as proof of superluminal communication. It is interpreted as the failure of local classical-property completeness. What is ruled out is the idea that A and B each carry a complete set of local, measurement-independent, pre-existing properties. MOR preserves relativistic causality while rejecting the classical ontological assumption that separation in macroscopic space entails independence in the microscopic relational domain.

## 7. The Measurement Problem, Single Outcomes, and Preferred Basis

MOR interprets collapse as slice factualization, but this does not automatically solve every aspect of the measurement problem. At least two issues must be openly acknowledged. First, the single-outcome problem: why is this slice factualized rather than another? Second, the preferred-basis problem: which slices count as candidates for factualization?

The weak version of MOR can take a conservative position. It accepts the Born rule as giving outcome weights, accepts decoherence as explaining pointer bases and stable records, and treats relative facts as primitive ontological facts in measurement events. This position is close to RQM, but MOR attempts to add explanatory structure through unmanifested domains and slice invariants.

If a future strong version is developed, MOR must explain the mechanism of slice factualization, the origin of the Born rule, and the selection of preferred bases. It cannot merely rename old problems using new terminology.

## 8. Wigner's Friend and Conflicts Between Relative Facts

Wigner's friend and related no-go theorems are a critical test for any theory of relative facts. Work by Frauchiger and Renner, Brukner, Bong, and others shows a deep tension among observer-independent facts, locality, and the universal validity of quantum mechanics.

MOR's basic response is that different observational reference frames may have different factual slices. The public projection plane is not the simple union of all slices, but a stable layer formed through interaction, decoherence, record comparison, and cross-reference-frame coordination. This, however, is only a programmatic answer. MOR must further explain which cross-slice links are allowed and which are not when the friend's factual slice conflicts with Wigner's slice of the entire laboratory.

For this reason, Wigner's friend should not be left for a future appendix. It should be treated as one of the core tests for any academic version of MOR.

## 9. Public Projection Plane and Objectivity

The public projection plane answers the following question: if facts are relative, why is the world still objectively stable? MOR's answer is that macroscopic objectivity does not arise from a reference-frame-free display of full-dimensional reality. It arises because large numbers of stable slices are redundantly recorded by the environment, amplified by instruments, and shared among observers.

This is highly related to quantum Darwinism. MOR should not present the public projection plane as if it had independently reinvented quantum Darwinism. Rather, it should acknowledge that the relevant physical mechanism comes from decoherence and redundant environmental records. MOR's addition is the claim that a public projection plane, even when stable, shared, and repeatable, remains a layer of manifestation rather than complete microscopic reality itself.

## 10. Spatial Emergence and the Wormhole Question

MOR's commitment to microscopic relational domains pushes the discussion toward the nature of spacetime. Macroscopic space may not be the original container of microscopic reality. It may be an emergent manifestation of microscopic relational domains within stable observational reference frames. This is suggestively related to spacetime-from-entanglement and ER=EPR research.

This paper does not, however, treat wormholes as a necessary explanation of entanglement, nor does it claim that ordinary entangled particles are connected by traversable wormholes. A more cautious statement is that wormholes suggest a possibility: some relations that appear distant in macroscopic space may not be genuinely separated in a deeper structure. Entanglement should first be understood as relational nonseparability. A wormhole is only a possible counterpart of such nonseparability in the language of gravitational geometry.

## 12. Boundaries, Risks, and Further Work

- MOR academic-v0.1 is not a new physical theory, but a draft reconstruction in quantum ontology.
- The Born rule is not derived; it is accepted as an empirical constraint.
- The unmanifested domain must not collapse into a set of local hidden variables.
- Slice factualization has not yet explained the mechanism selecting a single outcome.
- The public projection plane substantially overlaps with quantum Darwinism and must be positioned carefully.
- Wigner's friend and the coordination of facts across perspectives are necessary next steps.
- If the name "relativity" is retained, MOR must connect to QRF or another structure of reference-frame transformation.
- Spatial emergence and ER=EPR should remain suggestive directions, not established conclusions.

## Conclusion

The academic positioning of MOR academic-v0.1 is no longer "a wholly new quantum theory." It is an ontological reconstruction of relative facts, unmanifested domains, and slice invariants in the context of relational quantum mechanics and decoherence/quantum Darwinism. This positioning is more modest, and for that reason more likely to be read carefully.

If MOR has independent value, it does not lie in merely repeating that facts are relative or that the environment produces objectivity. It lies in posing three questions in a systematic way: should unfactualized structure have positive ontological status? Can intrinsic properties be understood as invariants across observational slices? Could macroscopic space be an emergent manifestation of microscopic relational domains within the public projection plane?

> The microscopic ground is first relational structure, not isolated entities. Measurement is first slice factualization, not a rupture of global reality. The macroscopic objective world is first a stably shared public projection plane, not a reference-frame-free display of full-dimensional reality.
